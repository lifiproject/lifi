About

What is Li-Fi?

Li-Fi technology, also known as Light Fidelity, is a wireless communication technology that uses visible light to transmit data. It is similar to Wi-Fi technology but instead of using radio waves, it uses LED lights to transmit data. This technology offers several advantages over traditional radio-based wireless communication technologies, including higher bandwidth, greater security, and no electromagnetic interference. Li-Fi has the potential to revolutionize the way we access the internet by providing faster and more secure wireless communication. It can be used in a variety of applications, including indoor navigation, smart homes, and industrial automation. While Li-Fi is still in the early stages of development, it has already shown promising results in laboratory settings and is expected to become more widely available in the coming years.

HOW DOES IT WORK?


  • DATA ENCODING
  • LED MODULATION
  • TRANSMISSION
  • RECEPTION
  • DATA ENCODING

DATA ENCODING

Data is first encoded into a format that can be transmitted using light waves. This is done by converting the data into binary code (0s and 1s). Then, the data is encoded into a series of light pulses, where each pulse corresponds to a 0 or a 1.

LED MODULATION

The binary data is then modulated from the light from an LED bulb to encode the digital information. The LED bulb is turned on and off very rapidly (millions of times per second) in accordance with the encoded data. This process is called on-off keying (OOK) modulation. The light that is emitted from the LED bulb carries the encoded data in the form of a light pulse sequence.

TRANSMISSION

The modulated light signal is transmitted through the air, just like a traditional radio wave in WiFi. The light waves are received by a photodetector, which detects the changes in the light signal and converts them back into electrical signals that correspond to the encoded data.

RECEPTION

The photodetector converts the light signal into electrical signals. These signals are then sent to a receiver that decodes the signals to extract the original digital data. The receiver may be a separate device or integrated into the photodetector.

DATA ENCODING

The binary data is decoded back into its original digital format. This is done by reversing the encoding process that was done on the sender side. The receiver side data decoding involves converting the electrical signals received by the receiver into binary code, which is then converted back into the original digital data.
